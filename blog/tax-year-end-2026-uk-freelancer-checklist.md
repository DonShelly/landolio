# Tax Year End 2026: Complete Checklist for UK Freelancers & Sole Traders

**The 2025/26 tax year ends on 5 April 2026.** That's your last chance to claim expenses, maximise tax relief, and reduce your tax bill for this financial year.

For UK freelancers and sole traders, tax year-end planning isn't optional — it's the difference between paying thousands less (or more) to HMRC.

**This guide covers everything you need to do before 5 April 2026** to legally minimise your tax bill and avoid costly mistakes.

---

## Why Tax Year-End Planning Matters

The UK tax year runs **6 April to 5 April**. Once 5 April passes, you can't backdate:
- Expense claims
- Pension contributions
- ISA contributions  
- Capital allowances
- Loss relief claims

**Example:** Sarah is a freelance designer. She earned £45,000 in 2025/26 but forgot to claim £8,000 of legitimate business expenses before 5 April 2026.

**Result:** She paid Income Tax + National Insurance on £45,000 instead of £37,000 — costing her approximately **£2,400 extra** in tax she can never reclaim.

**Bottom line:** Tax year-end planning is about claiming every penny you're entitled to **before the deadline passes**.

## The Tax Year-End Checklist (6 Weeks to Go)

### 1. Claim All Business Expenses (Before 5 April)

**What counts as a business expense?**
- Office costs (rent, utilities, equipment, software subscriptions)
- Travel (mileage, public transport, accommodation for business trips)
- Marketing (website hosting, ads, business cards)
- Professional fees (accountant, lawyer, professional memberships)
- Training (courses, books, conferences directly related to your business)

**How to maximise expense claims:**

**✅ Review your bank statements** for the entire tax year (April 2025 - March 2026). Look for:
- Recurring business subscriptions you forgot about
- One-off business purchases (equipment, software, furniture)
- Business mileage (45p per mile for first 10,000 miles, then 25p)

**✅ Claim home office expenses** if you work from home:
- **Simplified method:** £6/week (£312/year) — no receipts needed
- **Actual costs method:** Percentage of mortgage/rent, utilities, council tax, insurance based on office space

**✅ Don't miss these commonly forgotten expenses:**
- Mobile phone (business use portion)
- Internet (business use portion)
- Professional insurance
- Bank charges
- Bad debts (unpaid invoices you've written off)
- Repairs and maintenance

**Warning:** You must claim expenses **in the tax year they were incurred**. If you bought a laptop in March 2026, claim it before 5 April 2026. After that, you can't add it to your 2025/26 return.

### 2. Capital Allowances (Annual Investment Allowance)

If you bought equipment for your business (laptop, camera, van, machinery), you can claim **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA)** up to **£1 million per year**.

**How it works:**
- You bought a £2,000 laptop in March 2026
- Claim 100% of the cost (£2,000) as a capital allowance in 2025/26
- This reduces your taxable profit by £2,000

**Why it matters:**
- Basic rate taxpayer: Saves £400 in Income Tax + £200 in Class 4 NI = **£600 saved**
- Higher rate taxpayer: Saves £800 in Income Tax + £200 in Class 4 NI = **£1,000 saved**

**Strategic tip:** If you're planning to buy business equipment anyway, buy it **before 5 April 2026** to claim the tax relief this year instead of waiting until next year.

### 3. Pension Contributions (Triple Tax Relief)

Pension contributions are **one of the most tax-efficient ways** to reduce your tax bill.

**How it works:**
- You earn £50,000 profit in 2025/26
- You pay £5,000 into a personal pension before 5 April 2026
- HMRC reduces your taxable income to £45,000

**Tax relief:**
- **Basic rate (20%):** Save £1,000 in Income Tax + £450 in Class 4 NI = £1,450 total
- **Higher rate (40%):** Save £2,000 in Income Tax + £450 in Class 4 NI = £2,450 total

**Annual allowance:** You can contribute up to **£60,000** per year (or 100% of your earnings, whichever is lower) and receive full tax relief.

**Important:** Pension contributions must be made **before midnight on 5 April 2026** to count for the 2025/26 tax year.

**Strategic use:**
- If you're on the edge of the higher rate threshold (£50,270), a pension contribution can pull you back into the basic rate band
- If you're on the edge of losing your Personal Allowance (£100,000+), pension contributions can protect it

### 4. ISA Contributions (Tax-Free Savings)

ISAs (Individual Savings Accounts) let you shelter **£20,000** per year from tax.

**Why freelancers should care:**
- You're building up cash reserves for tax bills
- You're saving for business investments
- You're planning for slower months

**Put that money in an ISA** and earn interest **tax-free**.

**Example:** You have £15,000 in a regular savings account earning 5% interest = £750 interest per year. You pay 20%-45% Income Tax on that interest.

**Move it to a Cash ISA** and earn the full £750 tax-free.

**Deadline:** You must open and fund your ISA **before midnight on 5 April 2026**. The £20,000 allowance doesn't roll over — if you don't use it, you lose it.

### 5. Maximise Trading Allowance (£1,000 Tax-Free)

If you earn under £1,000 from self-employment, you **don't need to register for Self-Assessment** or pay tax on it. This is called the **Trading Allowance**.

**How it works:**
- You earned £800 from a side project in 2025/26
- You don't need to declare it or pay tax
- You don't need to register with HMRC

**If you earn over £1,000:**
- You must register for Self-Assessment
- You can **either**:
  - Deduct actual expenses from income (normal rules)
  - Claim the £1,000 Trading Allowance instead of expenses (if it's more)

**Strategic use:** If you have a small side project with minimal expenses, the £1,000 allowance might give you a bigger deduction than claiming actual expenses.

### 6. Capital Gains Tax Planning

If you sold assets in 2025/26 (property, shares, crypto, business assets), you may owe **Capital Gains Tax (CGT)**.

**CGT allowance 2025/26:** £3,000 tax-free

**Rates:**
- Basic rate taxpayer: 10% on gains (18% on property)
- Higher rate taxpayer: 20% on gains (24% on property)

**Strategic tips:**
- If you're planning to sell an asset, consider **splitting the sale** across two tax years to use two annual allowances
- If you're married/in a civil partnership, **transfer assets to your spouse** before selling to use both allowances (£6,000 total)

**Deadline:** Capital gains must be reported by **31 January 2027** (Self-Assessment deadline), but planning must happen **before you sell** the asset.

### 7. Loss Relief Claims

If your business made a **loss** in 2025/26, you can use that loss to reduce tax in other years.

**Options:**
1. **Carry forward the loss** to offset against future profits
2. **Carry back the loss** to the previous tax year (2024/25) and claim a tax refund
3. **Offset against other income** in 2025/26 (e.g., employment income, rental income)

**Example:** You made a £10,000 loss in 2025/26 but earned £30,000 in 2024/25 and paid £4,000 in tax.

You can **carry back the loss** to 2024/25, reducing your taxable income to £20,000, and claim a **£2,000+ tax refund** from HMRC.

**Deadline:** Loss relief claims must be made **within 4 years** of the end of the tax year. Don't wait — claim it in your 2025/26 Self-Assessment return.

### 8. VAT Threshold Planning (£90,000)

If your turnover is approaching **£90,000**, you must register for VAT. Once registered, you charge 20% VAT on your invoices and pay it to HMRC.

**Strategic planning:**
- If you're at £85,000 turnover, consider **deferring invoices** until after 5 April 2026 to stay below the threshold this year
- If you're at £92,000, you **must register** for VAT (or risk penalties)

**Deadline:** If you exceed £90,000 in any 12-month rolling period, you must notify HMRC **within 30 days** and register for VAT.

**VAT Flat Rate Scheme:** If you register, consider the Flat Rate Scheme — you charge 20% VAT but only pay 12%-14.5% to HMRC (depending on your sector), keeping the difference.

---

### 9. National Insurance Contributions (Class 2 + Class 4)

As a sole trader, you pay two types of National Insurance:

**Class 2 NI:**
- £3.45 per week (£179.40 per year)
- Only if your profits exceed £12,570
- Voluntary if profits are below £6,725 (but you should pay it to protect your State Pension)

**Class 4 NI:**
- 9% on profits between £12,570 - £50,270
- 2% on profits above £50,270

**Strategic tip:** If your profits are just over a threshold, **increasing pension contributions** can reduce your NI bill as well as your Income Tax.

---

### 10. Invoice Outstanding Clients (Before 5 April)

**If you do work before 5 April 2026**, it counts as 2025/26 income **even if you're not paid until later**.

**Action:**
- Send invoices for all work completed before 5 April 2026
- Chase outstanding invoices
- If clients won't pay, **write off bad debts** as an expense before 5 April

**Cash basis exception:** If you use cash basis accounting (most small businesses do), income only counts when you're **paid**, not invoiced. But you still need to chase payments to get cash in the bank.

## Tax Year-End Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Mistake 1: Waiting Until January 2027

**Problem:** Most freelancers wait until January (Self-Assessment deadline) to think about tax. By then, it's too late to claim expenses or make pension contributions for 2025/26.

**Solution:** Do your tax planning **now** (February/March), not January 2027.

---

### ❌ Mistake 2: Not Keeping Receipts

**Problem:** You claim £5,000 in expenses but have no receipts. HMRC investigates and disallows the claim. You owe back taxes + penalties + interest.

**Solution:** Keep digital copies of **every receipt** (photo on your phone, cloud storage, or accounting software).

---

### ❌ Mistake 3: Mixing Personal and Business Expenses

**Problem:** You claim your personal Netflix subscription as a business expense. HMRC investigates and discovers you're claiming non-business costs. Penalty: Up to 100% of the tax owed + criminal prosecution in serious cases.

**Solution:** Only claim expenses that are **wholly and exclusively** for business use. If something is mixed (e.g., mobile phone), claim the business-use percentage only.

---

### ❌ Mistake 4: Missing the Pension Contribution Deadline

**Problem:** You plan to pay £10,000 into your pension "soon" but miss the 5 April deadline. You lose £2,000-4,000 in tax relief.

**Solution:** Set up your pension contribution **now**. Don't wait until the last week — payment processors can be slow.

---

### ❌ Mistake 5: Not Using the £20,000 ISA Allowance

**Problem:** You have £25,000 sitting in a taxable savings account. You pay tax on the interest every year.

**Solution:** Move £20,000 into an ISA before 5 April 2026. Earn interest tax-free forever.

## FAQs

### When is the 2025/26 tax year-end deadline?
**5 April 2026** (midnight). After this date, you can't claim expenses, make pension contributions, or use your ISA allowance for the 2025/26 tax year.

### Can I backdate expenses after 5 April 2026?
**No.** Expenses must be claimed in the tax year they were incurred. If you bought something in March 2026, you must claim it in your 2025/26 Self-Assessment return.

### What happens if I miss the 5 April deadline?
You lose the opportunity to claim expenses, capital allowances, pension contributions, and ISA contributions for 2025/26. This could cost you thousands in extra tax.

### When do I need to file my Self-Assessment tax return?
**31 January 2027.** But planning must happen **before 5 April 2026** to maximise tax relief.

### Can I claim expenses without receipts?
**No.** HMRC can request proof of expenses. If you can't provide receipts, they'll disallow the claim and charge penalties + interest.

### Do I need an accountant?
Not legally required, but recommended if:
- Your finances are complex (multiple income sources, property, investments)
- You're not confident calculating tax
- You want to maximise tax relief

**DIY option:** Use accounting software (Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent) and file your own return.

### What's the difference between cash basis and accruals basis accounting?
- **Cash basis:** Income/expenses count when money is received/paid (simpler, most freelancers use this)
- **Accruals basis:** Income/expenses count when work is done/billed (regardless of payment)

### Can I change my accounting basis?
**Yes**, but you must stick with it for a full tax year. Notify HMRC when filing your Self-Assessment return.
