The Ultimate Freelancer Payment Protection Checklist

Everything you need to check before, during, and after every project

Why this checklist matters:

The average UK freelancer loses £3,500 per year to late or non-payment. This checklist has been built from lessons learned by thousands of freelancers who've been burned — so you don't have to be.

Print this out. Pin it to your wall. Check every box before you start work.

1

Before Taking the Job

  • Run a basic company check

    Search Companies House (UK) or equivalent. Check they're still trading, not in administration, and have filed recent accounts. Red flag: recently incorporated companies with no trading history.

  • Google the company + "payment issues" or "late payment"

    Check Trustpilot, Reddit, freelancer forums. If multiple people are complaining about payment, walk away.

  • Ask for a deposit (minimum 25-50%)

    If they refuse a deposit, that's a red flag. Legitimate businesses understand this protects both parties.

  • Get a written contract or statement of work

    Email confirmation is legally binding in the UK, but a proper contract is better. Must include: scope, deliverables, payment terms, timeline.

  • Include late payment terms in your contract

    UK: You're entitled to statutory interest (8% + Bank of England base rate) plus £40-£100 debt recovery fee. Put this in your terms.

  • Define "approval" and "completion" criteria

    Vague terms = scope creep = payment disputes. Be specific: "Client has 5 business days to request changes. After that, project is deemed approved."

  • Confirm payment method upfront

    Bank transfer, PayPal, Stripe? Get their preferred method in writing. If they say "we'll sort it later," push back.

  • Clarify who you're contracting with

    Is it the person you're talking to, or their company? Get the legal entity name and registration number. Chase payments from the right entity.

💡 Pro Tip: For projects over £1,000, ask for 50% upfront, 25% at midpoint, 25% on completion. This protects your cash flow and proves the client is serious.
2

During the Project

  • Document every scope change in writing

    Client asks for "just one more thing"? Email them: "To confirm, this is outside the original scope. I can do it for £X extra. Please reply to approve." Never do extra work without written approval.

  • Send progress updates at every milestone

    Even if they don't ask. "Phase 1 complete. Moving to Phase 2. Invoice #001 attached for £X." Keeps you top of mind and reduces surprise when invoices arrive.

  • Invoice immediately when each milestone is hit

    Don't wait until the end of the month. The sooner you invoice, the sooner you get paid. Most businesses pay within 30 days of invoice date — not project completion.

  • Track time and deliverables

    Even on fixed-price projects. If there's ever a dispute, you'll need proof of what you delivered and when.

  • Don't hand over final files until payment is confirmed

    Send watermarked previews, low-res versions, or demos. Once they have the files, your leverage disappears.

  • Get sign-off at every stage

    Email: "Phase 1 complete. Please confirm approval within 5 business days." If they don't respond, send a follow-up: "Taking your silence as approval to proceed."

  • Watch for red flags

    Sudden radio silence, vague feedback, "we'll pay when we have the budget," new people getting involved. If you see these, stop work until payment clears.

🚨 Stop-Work Trigger: If a milestone payment is more than 7 days overdue, pause all work immediately. Send a polite but firm email: "I'm pausing work until Invoice #X is settled. Happy to resume once payment clears." Don't be afraid to enforce this.
3

After Delivery

  • Send a professional invoice immediately

    Include: your business name, invoice number, date, payment terms (e.g., "Due within 14 days"), itemised breakdown, bank details, late payment terms.

  • Confirm receipt of the invoice

    Email: "Invoice #X sent. Can you confirm you've received it and when I can expect payment?" If they don't respond within 48 hours, follow up.

  • Set a calendar reminder for due date minus 3 days

    Send a friendly nudge: "Just a heads-up that Invoice #X is due on [date]. Let me know if you need any details to process it."

  • If payment is 1 day late, send a polite reminder

    "Hi [Name], Invoice #X was due yesterday. I'm sure it's just slipped through — can you check on it for me? Thanks!"

  • If payment is 7 days late, escalate tone slightly

    "Hi [Name], Invoice #X is now 7 days overdue. Please can you confirm when I can expect payment? As per our agreement, late fees apply from [date]."

  • If payment is 14 days late, send a formal demand

    "This is a formal request for payment of Invoice #X, now 14 days overdue. If payment is not received within 7 days, I will begin legal proceedings to recover the debt plus statutory interest and costs."

  • If payment is 21 days late, send a Letter Before Action

    Formal legal letter stating you'll take them to court if they don't pay within 14 days. Send via recorded delivery. (Free template available at landolio.com)

  • If payment is 35+ days late, file with Small Claims Court

    UK: Use Money Claim Online (gov.uk). Costs £25-£455 depending on claim value. You'll get your court fees back if you win.

💡 Pro Tip: Most late payments are resolved at the "7 days late" stage. Don't be afraid to follow up early and often. The squeaky wheel gets paid.
💼

Bonus: Payment Recovery Toolkit

  • Save all emails and contracts

    If you end up in court, you'll need proof of the agreement, deliverables, and payment terms. Keep everything.

  • Calculate statutory interest and recovery fees

    UK: 8% + BoE base rate on outstanding amount, plus £40 (under £1k), £70 (£1k-£10k), or £100 (over £10k). Use Landolio's free calculator.

  • Know your legal options

    UK: Letter Before Action → Small Claims Court → County Court Judgement → Bailiff enforcement. Each step escalates pressure.

  • Join freelancer communities

    Reddit r/freelance, UK Freelancers Facebook groups. Others have dealt with similar clients — learn from their experiences.

  • Consider payment protection insurance

    Protects against client insolvency. Costs 1-3% of invoice value. Worth it for large projects or risky clients.
