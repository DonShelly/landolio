Getting-Paid Checklist

A comprehensive, step-by-step checklist covering the entire payment lifecycle — from first contact to money in the bank.

1

Before You Start Work

Credit check the client (if it's a company)
Use Companies House or a credit checking service to verify the company is active, solvent, and has no CCJs. Red flags: dissolved status, multiple late filing penalties, or adverse court judgments.
Get everything in writing
Never start work on a verbal agreement. Use a written contract, signed statement of work, or at minimum a detailed email confirming scope, price, timeline, and payment terms.
Set clear payment terms upfront
State your payment terms explicitly: when invoices are due (e.g., "Net 14 days"), accepted payment methods, and late payment interest rate (UK statutory: 8% + Bank of England base rate).
Request a deposit for new clients
For first-time clients or large projects, ask for 25–50% upfront. This filters out time-wasters and protects you if they ghost mid-project.
Define scope clearly (and what's NOT included)
List exactly what you're delivering, how many revisions are included, and what's out of scope. Scope creep kills cash flow — protect yourself early.
Add a late payment clause to your contract
Include: "Late payments will incur interest at 8% + Bank of England base rate per annum, plus a £40 statutory debt recovery fee." Makes it legally enforceable.
Confirm who will approve and pay the invoice
Get the name and email of the person who authorises payment. Don't assume your day-to-day contact handles invoices — often they don't.
Ask about their payment cycle
Some companies only run invoices once a month. If you invoice the day after their cycle, you might wait 6 weeks. Ask upfront and plan accordingly.
Set project milestones with staggered payments
For large projects, break payments into milestones (e.g., 30% upfront, 40% at midpoint, 30% on completion). Never do all the work before getting paid anything.
Trust your gut — walk away from red flags
Pushy negotiation, vague scope, reluctance to sign contracts, or requests for spec work without pay? These are warning signs. Don't ignore them.
2

During the Project

Send invoices immediately upon delivery
Don't wait. The moment you deliver a milestone or complete the project, send the invoice. Delays = delayed payment. Strike while the value is fresh.
Make your invoice crystal clear
Include: invoice number, issue date, due date, payment terms, itemised breakdown, total amount, payment methods, and your VAT number (if registered). No ambiguity = faster payment.
Follow up immediately to confirm receipt
Send a polite "Just checking this reached you OK" email 1–2 days after sending. This confirms they received it and prompts them to queue payment.
Track payment due dates in a system
Use a spreadsheet, accounting software, or calendar reminder. Don't rely on memory. Know exactly when each invoice is due and set a reminder for the day after.
Send a friendly reminder 3 days before due date
"Just a friendly reminder that invoice #123 is due on [date]. Let me know if you need anything!" Proactive reminders prevent late payments.
Offer multiple payment methods
Bank transfer, PayPal, Stripe, credit card — the more options you offer, the fewer excuses they have. "We don't use that method" shouldn't block payment.
Document all scope changes with updated pricing
If they ask for extra work, send a written change order with the additional cost and revised timeline. Don't do free work hoping they'll "remember" later.
Keep communication professional and documented
Use email for all payment-related discussions. If they call, follow up with "As discussed..." confirmation email. You'll need this paper trail if things go south.
3

When Payment is Late

Day 1 overdue: Send a polite nudge
"Hi [Name], invoice #123 was due yesterday. Just checking it's in your system — let me know if you need me to resend!" Assume good intent. Mistakes happen.
Day 7 overdue: Formal but friendly chase
"Hi [Name], following up on invoice #123 (now 7 days overdue). Can you confirm when payment will be processed? Happy to help if there's an issue." Still polite, but firmer.
Day 14 overdue: Notify of late payment interest
"Invoice #123 is now 14 days overdue. As per our contract, late payment interest of [rate]% will apply from [date], plus a £40 statutory recovery fee." Make it clear there are consequences.
Day 21 overdue: Escalate to decision-maker
If your contact isn't responding, CC their manager, finance director, or company director. "I've tried to resolve this with [contact], but haven't received a response. Can you assist?"
Day 30 overdue: Send a Letter Before Action
Formal legal notice stating: amount owed, late fees, 7-day deadline to pay, and that you'll start court proceedings if not resolved. Send via email AND recorded delivery.
Day 37: File a claim via Money Claim Online
If they ignore your Letter Before Action, file a claim at gov.uk/make-money-claim. Costs £25–£455 depending on amount (usually recoverable). Most businesses pay at this stage.
If they dispute: Respond with evidence
Submit your contract, invoices, email trail, and proof of delivery. Courts favour freelancers with clear documentation. This is why you kept everything in writing.
If you win: Request a County Court Judgment (CCJ)
A CCJ damages their credit rating for 6 years and makes it harder for them to get credit. It's public record — Google-able. Most companies will pay to avoid this.
If they still don't pay: Enforce the judgment
Options: Bailiffs (£110 fee), Attachment of Earnings (if they're employed), Third Party Debt Order (freeze their bank account), or Charging Order (against property). Nuclear, but effective.
Consider selling the debt to a collection agency
You'll get 60–80% of the invoice value immediately, and they handle chasing. Good option if you want to move on and focus on paying clients.
Stay professional throughout (even when furious)
Never send angry emails, post on social media, or threaten illegal action. It damages your credibility and could be used against you in court. Be firm, not emotional.
Know when to cut your losses
If the debt is under £500 and enforcement will cost more than you'll recover, it might not be worth it. Learn from it, move on, and focus on clients who do pay.
4

Prevention for Next Time

Blacklist bad payers (and share warnings discreetly)
Keep a private list of clients who paid late or not at all. If fellow freelancers ask for references, warn them (tactfully). Protect your community.
Tighten your payment terms
Move from Net 30 to Net 14. Require 50% upfront for all new clients. Add an early payment discount (e.g., "2% off if paid within 7 days"). Make timely payment the path of least resistance.
Automate invoice follow-ups
Use tools like Landolio's Invoice Follow-Up Automator to send reminder sequences automatically. You shouldn't have to remember to chase — the system does it for you.
Build a 3-month emergency fund
Late payments hurt less when you have runway. Aim to save 3 months of living expenses so you're not desperate when someone pays 60 days late.
Diversify your client base
Don't rely on 1–2 big clients. If one goes bust or stops paying, you're in trouble. Spread risk across 5–10 clients so no single non-payment destroys your cash flow.
