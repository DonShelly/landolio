# IR35 Rules 2026: Complete Guide for UK Contractors — Inside vs Outside Explained

**Last updated: 26 February 2026**

If you're a UK contractor working through your own limited company, IR35 is probably the single biggest tax issue you'll face. Get it wrong, and you could face a 20-30% drop in take-home income, plus HMRC penalties and back-tax demands.

This guide explains everything you need to know about IR35 in 2026 — what it is, how to check your status, who's responsible for deciding, and how to protect yourself.

---

## What is IR35?

IR35 is HMRC's shorthand for **"disguised employment"** tax legislation. It's designed to catch contractors who are essentially employees but operate through a limited company (a Personal Service Company or PSC) to avoid PAYE tax and National Insurance.

**If you're caught inside IR35:**
- You pay income tax and National Insurance like an employee
- You lose the tax efficiency of dividends
- Your client (or agency) withholds tax at source
- Your take-home pay drops by 20-30%

**If you're genuinely outside IR35:**
- You operate as a true business
- You can pay yourself via dividends (more tax-efficient)
- You can claim legitimate business expenses
- You keep more of what you earn

The stakes are high: a contractor earning £60,000 outside IR35 might take home £45,000 after tax. The same person deemed inside IR35 could take home just £36,000 — a £9,000 annual difference.

## Inside vs Outside IR35: What's the Difference?

### Outside IR35 (Genuine Self-Employment)

You're running a **real business**:
- You control how, when, and where you work
- You can send a substitute if you're unavailable
- You're not obligated to accept work (and the client isn't obligated to offer it)
- You work for multiple clients (ideally)
- You provide your own equipment and tools
- You bear financial risk (your mistakes, your cost)

**Tax treatment:**
- Pay yourself a small salary (£12,570 tax-free allowance)
- Take profits as dividends (lower tax rates)
- Claim business expenses (equipment, travel, home office)
- File Self Assessment annually

### Inside IR35 (Disguised Employment)

You're effectively an **employee**:
- The client controls your working hours and location
- You must do the work personally (no substitutes)
- You're expected to work continuously (like a job)
- You use the client's equipment and systems
- The client bears the financial risk (you get paid either way)

**Tax treatment:**
- The "fee payer" (client or agency) operates PAYE
- Income tax and National Insurance deducted at source
- No dividend tax advantages
- Limited ability to claim expenses
- Effectively the same as being employed, but without employment rights

## The Three Key Tests: Control, Substitution, Mutuality of Obligation (MOO)

HMRC assesses IR35 status using three main tests. You need to pass **all three** to be genuinely outside IR35.

### 1. Control
**Question:** Who decides what work you do, how you do it, when you do it, and where you work?

**Outside IR35:**
- You control your working methods
- You decide your hours (as long as deadlines are met)
- You can work from anywhere (unless client site is essential)
- The client specifies the end result, not the process

**Inside IR35:**
- The client sets your working hours (e.g., "9am-5pm, Monday-Friday")
- You must work at the client's office
- You follow the client's processes and procedures
- You're supervised like an employee

**Red flags:**
- Mandatory attendance at team meetings
- Client sets your daily tasks
- You're given a company laptop and must use their systems
- You report to a line manager

### 2. Substitution (Personal Service)
**Question:** Can you send someone else to do the work if you're unavailable?

**Outside IR35:**
- You have an **unlimited right of substitution** (written into your contract)
- You can genuinely send a substitute without asking permission
- The client accepts substitutes (as long as they're competent)
- You've actually used a substitute before (best evidence)

**Inside IR35:**
- The contract says "you must perform the services personally"
- Any substitution requires client approval (which they can refuse)
- You've never sent a substitute (and don't have anyone lined up)

**Red flags:**
- Contract specifies you by name
- Client hired you for your specific expertise
- You have a company email address and building pass

### 3. Mutuality of Obligation (MOO)
**Question:** Is there an ongoing expectation that the client will offer work and you'll accept it?

**Outside IR35:**
- You work on **specific projects with defined end dates**
- The client has no obligation to offer more work after the project ends
- You have no obligation to accept new projects
- You can (and do) turn down work

**Inside IR35:**
- The engagement is **ongoing with no fixed end date**
- You're expected to be available continuously
- You'd be in breach if you refused work
- The relationship feels like a permanent job

**Red flags:**
- Contract auto-renews or extends repeatedly
- You've worked for the same client for 12+ months
- You're treated like part of the team (appraisals, team events)

## Who Decides Your IR35 Status in 2026?

This depends on **who you're working for** and **when the contract started**.

### Medium and Large Private Sector Clients
**Since April 2021:** The **client is responsible** for determining your IR35 status.

They must:
1. Use HMRC's CEST tool (or take professional advice)
2. Issue you a **Status Determination Statement (SDS)** before you start
3. Deduct tax at source if you're deemed inside IR35

**If the client is medium or large** (turnover over £15m, 50+ employees, or balance sheet over £18m), they carry the responsibility. If they get it wrong, they're liable for the tax.

### Small Private Sector Clients
**Since April 2025:** If your client is a **small company** (turnover under £15m), **you (the contractor) remain responsible** for the determination.

This means:
- You decide your own IR35 status
- You file Self Assessment accordingly
- If HMRC challenges you, it's your liability

### Public Sector Clients
**Since April 2017:** The **public sector organisation is always responsible** for determining your status. You have no say.

## How to Check Your IR35 Status: The CEST Tool

HMRC provides a free online tool called **CEST** (Check Employment Status for Tax). It asks a series of questions about your contract and working arrangements, then gives a determination.

**Link:** [gov.uk/guidance/check-employment-status-for-tax](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/check-employment-status-for-tax)

### What CEST Asks

You'll need to know:
- **Contract details:** Is there a written contract? What does it say about substitution?
- **Control:** Who decides what work is done, when, where, and how?
- **Financial risk:** Who provides equipment? Who bears the cost of mistakes?
- **Mutuality of Obligation:** Is there an ongoing expectation of work?
- **Business on own account:** Do you work for multiple clients? Do you market your services?

### CEST Results

The tool gives one of three results:
1. **Outside IR35** — you're self-employed for tax purposes
2. **Inside IR35** — you're employed for tax purposes (PAYE applies)
3. **Unable to determine** — the situation is too complex (seek professional advice)

**Important:** HMRC will stand by the tool's result, as long as the information you provide is accurate. Save the result as evidence.

### CEST Limitations

CEST is helpful, but not perfect:
- It can't capture all the nuances of your situation
- If you get "unable to determine," you're still at risk
- It doesn't replace professional IR35 advice (especially for high earners)

**Best practice:** Use CEST as a starting point, then get a professional review for anything marginal or high-value.

## How to Stay Outside IR35: Practical Steps

If you want to remain genuinely self-employed, you need to structure your contracts and working practices carefully.

### 1. Negotiate Right of Substitution
- Get **unlimited substitution** written into your contract
- Don't accept clauses like "substitutes subject to client approval"
- Keep a list of potential substitutes (even if you never use them)
- If possible, actually send a substitute once to prove it's real

### 2. Demonstrate Control Over Your Work
- Work remotely when possible (don't be office-based full-time)
- Set your own hours (as long as deadlines are met)
- Don't attend regular team meetings (project kick-offs are fine)
- Refuse company email addresses and building passes

### 3. Avoid Mutuality of Obligation
- Work on **fixed-term projects** (3-6 months max)
- Don't auto-renew contracts — let them end, then negotiate new ones
- Turn down work occasionally (if you can afford to)
- Work for multiple clients simultaneously (ideally)

### 4. Bear Financial Risk
- Provide your own laptop and equipment
- Have Professional Indemnity Insurance (shows you're a real business)
- Accept fixed-price contracts where possible (not just day rates)
- Don't claim expenses from the client — invoice and pay yourself

### 5. Act Like a Business
- Have a business website (even a simple one)
- Market your services (LinkedIn, networking)
- Have multiple clients over the course of a year
- Keep detailed records (contracts, invoices, working arrangements)

## What to Do If You're Deemed Inside IR35

If a client determines you're inside IR35 (or you determine it yourself for a small client), here's what happens:

### 1. The Fee Payer Operates PAYE
- The client (or agency in the supply chain) deducts tax and NI at source
- You receive net pay (after tax)
- They issue you a payslip and P60 at year-end

### 2. You Lose Tax Efficiency
- No more dividend tax advantages
- Limited ability to claim expenses
- Your limited company becomes a "hollow shell" for that contract

### 3. You Can Still Operate Your PSC
- You can still invoice through your company
- You can still have other clients outside IR35
- Your company just becomes a pass-through for the inside-IR35 contract

### 4. You Have a Right to Challenge
If you think the client's determination is wrong, you can:
- Request a review (clients must have a disputes process)
- Provide evidence (contract, working practices, CEST result)
- Get professional IR35 advice to support your case
- Walk away if they won't reconsider (if you can afford to)

### 5. Plan Your Next Move
If you're stuck inside IR35 for one contract:
- Finish the project and move on
- Look for clients who'll engage you outside IR35
- Restructure your working practices for future contracts
- Consider fixed-price project work instead of day-rate contracting

## IR35 Changes Coming in 2026

HMRC is always tweaking the rules. Here's what contractors need to watch for in 2026:

### 1. Company Size Thresholds May Change
The threshold for "small companies" (currently under £15m turnover) could be revised. If it drops, more contractors will become responsible for their own determinations.

### 2. Umbrella Company Reforms
The government is introducing new regulations for umbrella companies (used by contractors deemed inside IR35). Watch for:
- New compliance requirements
- Supply chain liability changes
- Potential HMRC crackdowns on non-compliant umbrellas

### 3. Increased HMRC Scrutiny
HMRC is investing in IR35 enforcement. Expect:
- More status enquiries (especially for long-term single-client engagements)
- Retrospective tax demands if you get it wrong
- Penalties for clients who don't follow the rules

### 4. Case Law Developments
IR35 is constantly tested in court. Recent cases (e.g., Atholl House, Kaye Adams) are shaping how the rules are interpreted. Stay updated on case law developments.

## Common IR35 Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake 1: Relying on the Contract Alone
**Why it's wrong:** HMRC looks at **actual working practices**, not just what the contract says.

**Fix:** Ensure your day-to-day work matches the contract. If it says you have substitution rights, be ready to actually send a substitute.

### Mistake 2: Working for One Client for Years
**Why it's wrong:** Long-term single-client engagements scream "disguised employment."

**Fix:** Take breaks between contracts. Work for multiple clients. Keep projects under 12 months.

### Mistake 3: Accepting a Client's Inside IR35 Determination Without Challenge
**Why it's wrong:** Clients often default to "inside IR35" to avoid liability — even if you're genuinely outside.

**Fix:** Challenge incorrect determinations. Provide evidence. Walk away if necessary.

### Mistake 4: Not Keeping Evidence
**Why it's wrong:** If HMRC challenges you, you need proof of your working arrangements.

**Fix:** Save contracts, emails showing flexibility, evidence of substitutes, records of multiple clients.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring "Unable to Determine" CEST Results
**Why it's wrong:** "Unable to determine" means you're in a grey area — HMRC might still challenge you.

**Fix:** Get professional IR35 advice. Restructure the contract. Document everything.

## IR35 Checklist: Are You Genuinely Outside IR35?

Use this checklist to assess your status:

**Contract:**
- [ ] Right of substitution clearly stated (unlimited, no client approval needed)
- [ ] No "personal service" clause requiring you to do the work
- [ ] Fixed-term project (not ongoing/permanent)
- [ ] Client has no obligation to offer work; you have no obligation to accept

**Working Practices:**
- [ ] You control when and where you work (within project deadlines)
- [ ] You use your own equipment (laptop, phone, software)
- [ ] You don't have a company email address or building pass
- [ ] You're not supervised like an employee

**Business Reality:**
- [ ] You work (or have worked) for multiple clients in the past 12 months
- [ ] You market your services (website, LinkedIn, networking)
- [ ] You have Professional Indemnity Insurance
- [ ] You bear financial risk (fixed-price work, liability for mistakes)

**HMRC CEST:**
- [ ] You've run your contract through CEST and got "outside IR35"
- [ ] You've saved the result as evidence
- [ ] The information you provided was accurate and honest

**If you ticked all (or most) boxes:** You're likely genuinely outside IR35. Keep evidence.

**If you ticked fewer than 10:** You're in a grey area. Get professional advice.

**If you ticked fewer than 5:** You're probably inside IR35. Prepare for PAYE treatment.

## What If HMRC Challenges Your IR35 Status?

If HMRC opens an IR35 enquiry, here's what to expect:

### 1. HMRC Contacts You
You'll receive a letter asking for information about your contracts and working arrangements.

### 2. They Review Your Contracts and Working Practices
HMRC will look at:
- Contracts (written terms)
- Emails and correspondence (evidence of control)
- Invoices and payment records
- Evidence of substitutes, multiple clients, business-like operation

### 3. They Make a Determination
HMRC will decide if you're inside or outside IR35 for specific contracts.

### 4. If You're Deemed Inside IR35
You'll receive a tax demand for:
- Unpaid PAYE and National Insurance
- Penalties (up to 100% of the tax owed for deliberate errors)
- Interest on late payment

### 5. You Can Appeal
If you disagree:
- Challenge the determination with evidence
- Request an HMRC review
- Take it to a tax tribunal (if necessary)

**Best defence:** Detailed records of working practices, CEST results, professional advice.

## IR35 and Different Types of Contractors

IR35 applies differently depending on your sector and work type:

### IT Contractors
**High risk:** Often work on long-term projects at client sites, using client equipment.

**Mitigation:** Work remotely, keep contracts under 12 months, demonstrate technical autonomy.

### Construction Contractors
**Separate rules:** If you're in construction, [CIS (Construction Industry Scheme)](/blog/construction-subcontractors-mtd-guide-2026) applies. IR35 can apply on top of CIS.

**Key issue:** Site-based work often fails the control test. Ensure you control your methods.

### Creative/Marketing Contractors
**Medium risk:** Often project-based (good), but can become embedded in client teams (bad).

**Mitigation:** Work for multiple clients, deliver defined outputs, avoid "retainer" arrangements.

### Finance/Legal Contractors
**High risk:** Often interim roles that look like temporary employment.

**Mitigation:** Take on transformation projects (not BAU), maintain professional autonomy.

## Final Thoughts: IR35 is About Reality, Not Paperwork

The most important thing to understand about IR35 is this:

**HMRC doesn't care what your contract says. They care how you actually work.**

You can have the most beautifully crafted IR35-proof contract in the world — but if you turn up to the client's office 9-5 every day, sit in team meetings, and work like an employee, you're inside IR35.

Conversely, if your contract is marginal but you genuinely operate as a business (multiple clients, substitutes, control over your work, financial risk), you're probably outside IR35.

**The key is alignment:** Your contract and your working practices must match. Both must demonstrate genuine self-employment.

If you're in doubt, get professional advice. The cost of an IR35 review (£200-500) is trivial compared to a retrospective tax demand (potentially tens of thousands).
