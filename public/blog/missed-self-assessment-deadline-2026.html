Missed Self-Assessment Deadline 2026? Here's What Happens Next

Published 24 February 2026 · 8 min read

⏰ Time-sensitive: If you missed the 31 January 2026 deadline, you've already been charged a £100 penalty. But it gets worse — on 1 May 2026, daily penalties of £10/day kick in. Read this guide now to understand what happens next and how to minimize damage.

What You Need to Know Right Now

The Self-Assessment deadline for the 2024/25 tax year was 11:59 PM on 31 January 2026. If you missed it, here's the situation:

  • You've already been penalized £100 — this is automatic, even if you owe no tax
  • Interest is accruing daily on any unpaid tax since 1 February 2026
  • Further penalties are coming if you don't file soon
  • Filing late doesn't remove the penalty — but it stops it getting worse

Example: If you owe £5,000 in tax and file 7 months late, your penalties would be:
  • £100 (immediate)
  • £900 (90 days × £10/day)
  • £300 (6-month penalty — £300 is greater than 5% × £5,000 = £250)
  • Total penalties: £1,300
  • Plus interest on the £5,000 tax bill

What Happens Next (Timeline)

Now – 30 April 2026

You're in the "£100 only" zone. File your return ASAP to avoid the £10/day penalties starting 1 May. Even if you can't pay the tax yet, filing stops the penalties escalating.

Action: File online immediately. Set up a payment plan with HMRC if you can't pay in full.

1 May 2026

Daily penalties start. £10 per day for the next 90 days (until 29 July 2026). That's £900 total if you don't file before 1 August.

Priority: File before 1 May to avoid this entirely. If you miss it, file ASAP to stop the clock.

1 August 2026

6-month penalty kicks in. £300 or 5% of tax owed (whichever is greater). This is on top of the £100 + daily penalties you've already racked up.

At this point: You've paid £100 + £900 + £300 = £1,300 in penalties alone (if you owe more than £6,000 tax, the 5% penalty will be higher).

1 February 2027

12-month penalty. Another £300 or 5% of tax owed. Total penalties now: £100 + £900 + £300 + £300 = £1,600 minimum (plus ongoing interest).

Your Action Plan (Do This Today)

Step 1: File Your Return Immediately

Even if you're missing information or can't pay the tax yet — file anyway. You can amend the return later if needed, but filing stops the penalties from escalating.

  • Online filing: Log in to HMRC Self-Assessment online
  • Missing info? Use your best estimate. HMRC prefers an estimated return to no return.
  • Already filed but paid late? Interest accrues but filing penalties stop.

Step 2: Set Up a Payment Plan (If You Can't Pay in Full)

HMRC offers "Time to Pay" arrangements — you can spread the cost over up to 12 months if you:

  • Owe less than £30,000
  • Have filed your return
  • Have no other payment plans or debts with HMRC

How to set it up: Use the HMRC payment plan service online (available after you've filed).

Important: Setting up a Time to Pay agreement suspends late payment penalties — but you still pay interest on the outstanding balance.

Step 3: Appeal the £100 Penalty (If You Have Grounds)

You can appeal the initial £100 penalty if you have a reasonable excuse. HMRC accepts:

  • Serious illness or bereavement
  • Fire, flood, or theft preventing you from filing
  • Postal delays beyond your control (paper returns only)
  • HMRC's systems being down at the deadline

Not accepted: "I forgot," "I was busy," "I didn't know the deadline," or "My accountant didn't do it."

How to appeal: Write to HMRC within 30 days of receiving the penalty notice. Explain your situation and provide evidence (e.g., doctor's note).

Common Mistakes People Make

❌ "I'll wait until I have all the paperwork"

Wrong. File with estimates now. You can amend later. Waiting costs you £10/day from 1 May onwards.

❌ "I can't pay, so there's no point filing"

Wrong. Filing stops the penalties escalating. Not filing costs you £900 in daily penalties (May-July) even if you owe £0 tax.

❌ "I'll appeal the penalty after I file"

Backwards. File first, then appeal. Appeals can take months — filing stops the clock immediately.

❌ "I didn't get a reminder, so it's HMRC's fault"

Doesn't work. HMRC doesn't send reminders. The deadline is fixed by law — not knowing isn't a reasonable excuse.

How to Avoid This Nightmare Next Year

The 2025/26 tax year deadline is 31 January 2027. Here's how to make sure you're not reading this article again next year:

  1. Set up a self-assessment calendar — monthly reminders to collect receipts, invoices, bank statements
  2. File early — aim for December instead of January. Gives you breathing room if something goes wrong.
  3. Use software — tools like FreeAgent, QuickBooks, or Xero automate most of the record-keeping
  4. Set aside tax monthly — 20-30% of your income into a separate account. When the bill comes, you're ready.
  5. Get professional help — if you're consistently late or confused, hire an accountant. Costs £300-500 but saves you £1,000+ in penalties.
