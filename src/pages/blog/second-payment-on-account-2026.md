# Second Payment on Account 2026: Complete Guide for UK Freelancers

**Deadline: 31 July 2026 at midnight**

If you're self-employed or file a Self-Assessment tax return, you'll likely need to make a **second payment on account** by 31 July 2026. Miss it, and you'll face automatic penalties and daily interest charges.

This guide explains what payments on account are, who needs to pay them, how much you'll owe, and how to avoid penalties.

---

## What Are Payments on Account?

Payments on account are **advance payments** towards your next tax bill. Instead of paying all your tax in one go on 31 January, HMRC splits the bill into three parts:

1. **First payment on account** — due 31 January (same day as your tax return)
2. **Second payment on account** — due 31 July
3. **Balancing payment** — due 31 January the following year (if you owe more than the two advance payments)

Each payment on account is **50% of your previous year's tax bill** (excluding any tax already deducted at source like PAYE or CIS).

### Example

- Your 2024/25 tax bill (filed January 2026) was £8,000
- **31 January 2026:** You paid £4,000 (first payment on account for 2025/26) + £8,000 (balancing payment for 2024/25) = **£12,000 total**
- **31 July 2026:** You owe £4,000 (second payment on account for 2025/26)
- **31 January 2027:** You file your 2025/26 return and pay any balance

---

## Who Needs to Make Payments on Account?

You need to make payments on account if your **Self-Assessment tax bill was £1,000 or more** in the previous year, **and** less than 80% of your total tax was deducted at source (PAYE, CIS, etc.).

### You DON'T need to pay on account if:
- Your previous year's tax bill was under £1,000
- More than 80% of your tax was deducted at source (e.g., you have a PAYE job alongside freelancing)
- Your income dropped significantly and you've applied to reduce your payments

### Common situations:

| Situation | Payment on Account? |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Pure freelancer earning £40k+ profit | ✅ Yes |
| Freelancer + PAYE job (most tax via PAYE) | ❌ Probably not |
| Construction subcontractor (CIS deducted) | ❌ Probably not (80% rule) |
| First year self-employed (no prior tax bill) | ❌ Not yet (starts year 2) |
| Landlord with rental income | ✅ Yes |
| Sole trader with low profits (<£15k) | ❌ Probably under £1k threshold |

**Not sure?** Check your previous year's tax calculation (SA302). If payments on account are listed, you owe them.

## How Much Do You Owe?

Your second payment on account is **50% of last year's total tax bill** (excluding any tax deducted at source).

### How to Calculate It:

1. Find your previous year's tax bill (from your SA302 or tax calculation)
2. Subtract any tax already deducted at source (PAYE, CIS, etc.)
3. Divide by 2

**Example 1: Pure Freelancer**
- Previous year's tax bill: £6,000
- Tax deducted at source: £0
- Payment on account: £6,000 ÷ 2 = **£3,000 due 31 July**

**Example 2: Freelancer + PAYE**
- Previous year's tax bill: £8,000
- Tax deducted via PAYE: £5,000
- Remaining tax: £3,000
- Payment on account: £3,000 ÷ 2 = **£1,500 due 31 July**

**Example 3: Subcontractor (CIS)**
- Previous year's tax bill: £4,000
- CIS deducted: £3,500
- Remaining tax: £500
- **No payment on account required** (under £1,000)

### Where to Find Your Amount:

1. Log in to HMRC online account: [www.gov.uk/personal-tax-account](https://www.gov.uk/personal-tax-account)
2. Go to "Self Assessment" → "View Account"
3. Look for "Payment on account 2 of 2" under "What you owe"

## Payment Methods

You can pay via:

- **Debit card** (online) — instant confirmation
- **Bank transfer** — allow 3-5 working days
- **Direct Debit** — set up in advance
- **CHAPS** (same-day) — for last-minute payments
- **Cheque** — must arrive before deadline (not recommended)

**UTR and payment reference:** You'll need your **Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR)** to pay. Find it on your tax return or HMRC correspondence.

### Important:
- **Allow 3-5 working days** for bank transfers to clear
- Paying on 31 July by bank transfer is too late (will arrive August)
- Debit card or CHAPS are safest for last-minute payments

## What If You Can't Afford It?

If you can't pay by 31 July, you have options:

### 1. Apply to Reduce Your Payments on Account

If your income has dropped this year, you can **apply to reduce your payments on account** before the deadline.

**When to do this:**
- Your income is significantly lower than last year
- You've changed jobs or lost a client
- You're claiming more expenses this year

**How to apply:**
- Log in to HMRC online account
- Go to "Self Assessment" → "Request a reduction"
- Provide details of your expected income

**Warning:** If you reduce too much and still owe tax in January, HMRC may charge interest on the underpaid amount.

### 2. Set Up a Time to Pay Arrangement

Can't reduce your payment but can't afford the full amount? You can request a **payment plan** with HMRC.

**Eligibility:**
- You owe £30,000 or less
- You have no other HMRC debts
- Your tax return is up to date

**How to apply:**
- Call HMRC: **0300 200 3822**
- Or apply online via your HMRC account (if eligible)
- You'll need to explain why you can't pay and propose a payment schedule

**Typical terms:**
- 3-12 month payment plans available
- Interest charged at 7.75% (HMRC base rate + 2.5%)
- No upfront penalty if you stick to the plan

### 3. Pay Late (Not Recommended)

If you pay late without arranging a payment plan:
- **5% penalty** on unpaid amounts if still unpaid 30 days after deadline (30 August)
- **Additional 5% penalty** at 6 months overdue (31 January 2027)
- **Additional 5% penalty** at 12 months overdue (31 July 2027)
- **Daily interest** (currently 7.75%) from 1 August 2026

**Example:**
- You owe £3,000 but don't pay
- 30 August 2026: £150 penalty (5%)
- 31 January 2027: £150 penalty (5% again)
- 31 July 2027: £150 penalty (5% again)
- **Total:** £3,450 + 12 months of daily interest

## How to Avoid Penalties

### 1. Calendar the Deadline

Put **31 July 2026** in your calendar **now**. Set a reminder for mid-July to check your HMRC account.

### 2. Check What You Owe Early

Don't wait until July to check. Log in to your HMRC account in June and confirm the amount.

### 3. Budget for It

Payments on account can catch people off guard. If you owed £6,000 in January 2026, you'll owe another £3,000 in July — **plan for it**.

**Pro tip:** Set aside **30% of every invoice** into a separate tax savings account. When July comes, you'll have the cash ready.

### 4. Reduce Early If Income Has Dropped

If your income is genuinely lower this year, apply to reduce your payment **before the deadline**. Don't wait until you're already in debt.

### 5. Set Up a Payment Plan BEFORE the Deadline

If you know you can't pay, call HMRC before 31 July. Payment plans set up before the deadline avoid the 5% penalty.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ "I already paid in January — I'm done"
No. If you made a payment on account in January, you still owe a second payment in July.

### ❌ "I'll pay when I file my return next January"
The second payment on account is **due 31 July 2026**, not January 2027. Waiting until January means 6 months of penalties and interest.

### ❌ "I paid late last time and nothing happened"
HMRC penalties are automatic. You might not see them immediately, but they're added to your account and will be chased.

### ❌ "I'll reduce my payment to £0 to avoid paying"
HMRC may charge interest if you reduce too much and still owe tax in January. Only reduce if your income genuinely dropped.

### ❌ "I'll pay by bank transfer on 31 July"
Bank transfers take 3-5 working days. Pay by 26 July to be safe, or use debit card/CHAPS for same-day payment.

## What Happens After You Pay?

Once you've made your second payment on account:

1. **Payment clears** — usually within 3-5 working days (instant for debit card)
2. **HMRC updates your account** — check online to confirm zero balance
3. **You're done until January 2027** — when you file your 2025/26 return and pay any balancing payment

### Balancing Payment (31 January 2027)

When you file your 2025/26 tax return in January 2027:
- You'll have already paid two payments on account (£X each)
- If your actual tax bill is higher, you pay the difference (balancing payment)
- If your actual tax bill is lower, HMRC refunds the overpayment
- Plus: you'll make your first payment on account for 2026/27 (based on your 2025/26 bill)

**Example:**
- You paid £3,000 in January 2026 (first payment on account)
- You paid £3,000 in July 2026 (second payment on account)
- Your actual 2025/26 tax bill is £7,000
- **31 January 2027:** You owe £1,000 (balancing payment) + £3,500 (first payment on account for 2026/27) = **£4,500**

## FAQs

### Do I have to make payments on account if I'm newly self-employed?
Not in your first year. Payments on account only start in your second year (based on your first year's tax bill).

### Can I pay more than the required amount?
Yes. Extra payments reduce your January 2027 bill. You can make voluntary payments via your HMRC account.

### What if I overpaid last year and got a refund?
If your actual tax bill was lower than your payments on account, HMRC refunds the difference. But your next year's payments on account are still based on your actual bill (not the overpayment).

### Can I pay in installments?
Not automatically. You can request a Time to Pay arrangement, which lets you spread payments over 3-12 months.

### What if I forgot to file my tax return but still made payments on account?
Payments on account are separate from your tax return filing deadline. You'll still owe late filing penalties if you missed 31 January, but your payments reduce the amount you owe.
