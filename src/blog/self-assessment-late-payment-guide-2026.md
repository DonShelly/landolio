# Self-Assessment Late Payment Guide 2026: Interest, Penalties & How to Pay

The 31 January 2026 Self-Assessment deadline has passed. If you haven't paid your tax bill yet, you're now in HMRC's late payment zone — but you're not alone. Around 30% of UK sole traders pay late every year.

Here's exactly what happens now, what it costs you, and how to minimise the damage.

## What You Owe Right Now

### Late Payment Interest
**Starts:** 1 February 2026 (day after payment deadline)
**Rate:** 7.75% per year (as of Jan 2026)
**Charged on:** Your outstanding tax bill
**Frequency:** Daily until paid

If you owe £2,000 and pay 30 days late:
- Daily interest: £0.42
- Total 30-day interest: £12.74

Interest is non-negotiable. HMRC charges it automatically.

### Late Payment Penalties (5% Surcharges)
These are separate from interest:

| When | Penalty | Example (£2,000 bill) |
|------|---------|----------------------|
| 30 days late (2 March 2026) | 5% of unpaid tax | £100 |
| 6 months late (1 August 2026) | Additional 5% | £100 |
| 12 months late (1 February 2027) | Additional 5% | £100 |

**Important:** These 5% penalties are ON TOP of interest. They're not a substitute.

So if you owe £2,000 and pay 31 days late:
- Interest: ~£13
- 5% penalty: £100
- **Total cost: £113**

## Daily £10 Penalties (Different from Late Payment)

**These are filing penalties, not payment penalties.** If you haven't submitted your tax return yet, you face:

| When | Penalty |
|------|---------|
| 1 day late | £100 flat fee (immediate) |
| 3 months late (1 May 2026) | £10 per day (up to 90 days = £900 max) |
| 6 months late | £300 or 5% of tax (whichever is higher) |
| 12 months late | £300 or 5% of tax (whichever is higher) |

**Check your status:** Have you FILED your return? If yes, you only face late payment penalties. If no, you face BOTH filing and payment penalties.

## How Interest is Calculated

HMRC uses a daily compound calculation:

```
Daily interest = (Outstanding amount × Annual rate) ÷ 365
```

Example: £5,000 tax bill, 60 days late (7.75% rate)
- Daily interest: £1.06
- Total 60-day interest: £63.84

**The longer you wait, the faster it compounds.** Paying £100/month is cheaper than paying £1,200 in one go after 12 months (because interest stops accruing on the paid portion).

## Payment Options When You're Late

### 1. Pay in Full Immediately
**Best option if you can afford it.**

Payment methods:
- **Bank transfer (Faster Payments):** Clears same day if sent before 4pm
- **Debit card (online):** Instant, no fee
- **CHIC (Clearing House Automated Payment System):** Free but takes 3 working days
- **At your bank:** Same day (take your payment reference)

**Get your payment reference:** Log into HMRC online → Self-Assessment → View account → "Make a payment"

### 2. Time to Pay Arrangement (Payment Plan)
**If you can't pay in full, set up a payment plan.**

**Eligibility:**
- Owe less than £30,000
- Tax return already filed
- No other HMRC payment plans active
- No other HMRC debts

**How it works:**
- You propose monthly payments (up to 12 months usually)
- Interest keeps accruing (7.75% annual rate)
- 5% penalties still apply at 30 days, 6 months, 12 months
- Set up online via HMRC Self-Assessment account

**Important:** The 5% penalties are charged on your ORIGINAL bill, not on the remaining balance. So even if you pay down to £500, you still owe 5% of the full amount at each milestone.

**Example:** £3,000 bill, payment plan over 6 months
- Month 1-6: Pay £500/month
- Interest accrues monthly (~£12/month initially, decreasing)
- 30-day penalty (2 March): £150 (5% of £3,000)
- 6-month penalty (1 August): £150 (5% of £3,000)
- **Total cost: £3,000 + £150 + £150 + ~£50 interest = £3,350**

### 3. Partial Payment
**Pay what you can now, arrange plan for the rest.**

Even £500 immediately reduces your interest-accruing balance. Then set up a Time to Pay for the remainder.

## How to Set Up a Time to Pay Arrangement

### Online (Fastest)
1. Log into HMRC Self-Assessment online
2. Go to "Pay your Self-Assessment tax bill"
3. Select "Set up a payment plan"
4. Enter your proposed monthly amount
5. HMRC reviews instantly (most approved automatically)
6. First payment taken immediately

**Requirements:**
- Must be within 60 days of payment deadline (by 31 March 2026)
- Minimum £32/month
- Must be able to pay in full within 12 months

### Phone (For Complex Cases)
Call HMRC Payment Support: **0300 200 3822**
**Hours:** Monday-Friday, 8am-6pm

**Bring:**
- Your Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR)
- National Insurance number
- Details of your income/expenses
- Proposed payment amount

HMRC may ask why you can't pay in full. Be honest: "unexpected expense", "business income delay", "cash flow issue". They don't need details.

### What if They Reject Your Plan?
HMRC may reject if:
- Proposed payments are too low
- You have previous payment plan defaults
- You owe more than £30,000

**Options:**
- Increase your monthly amount
- Pay a lump sum upfront, then arrange plan for remainder
- Contact a tax advisor (accountant or Citizen's Advice)

## Avoiding Future Late Payments

### 1. Set Up a Payment on Account
If your Self-Assessment bill was over £1,000 this year, HMRC will require **Payments on Account** for 2026-27:

- **31 January 2027:** Pay 50% of this year's bill
- **31 July 2027:** Pay remaining 50%
- **31 January 2028:** Pay balance (difference between actual and estimated)

This spreads the pain but means you're paying for last year + this year at the same time.

### 2. Monthly Tax Savings
Open a separate savings account. Every time you invoice:
- Multiply by your effective tax rate (20%-45% depending on income)
- Transfer that amount to tax savings immediately
- Don't touch it until 31 January

**Example:** You invoice £1,000. Transfer £250 (25%) to tax savings. When January arrives, you have £3,000 saved automatically.

### 3. Register for Making Tax Digital (MTD)
From April 2026, sole traders earning over £50,000 must file quarterly via MTD-compatible software.

**Benefits:**
- Quarterly submissions = no year-end panic
- Real-time tax estimates (know what you owe throughout the year)
- Automatic reminders

## What Not to Do

### ❌ Ignore HMRC Letters
They escalate fast:
1. Payment reminder (polite)
2. Final notice (threat of enforcement)
3. Debt collection agency
4. County Court Judgement (CCJ)
5. Bailiffs / Enforcement agents

**Timeline:** 2-3 months from first missed payment to CCJ.

### ❌ Wait for the 5% Penalty to Hit
Interest is 7.75% annually. The 5% penalty hits at 30 days. Waiting to see if you can scrape together funds in 29 days costs you an extra £100+ (on a £2,000 bill).

Pay something immediately. Even £200 reduces your interest burden.

### ❌ Use a Credit Card for Tax Bills
HMRC doesn't accept credit cards. Some people use a credit card cash advance to pay HMRC via bank transfer.

**Don't do this unless:**
- Credit card interest is LOWER than HMRC interest (unlikely — typical credit card APR is 20%+)
- You can pay off the credit card within 2-3 months

HMRC's 7.75% is cheaper than most credit cards.

## Special Circumstances

### Genuine Inability to Pay
If you're facing genuine hardship (job loss, business collapse, health crisis), call HMRC Payment Support and explain.

They may:
- Extend your Time to Pay beyond 12 months
- Reduce or waive penalties (not interest — that's automatic)
- Refer you to debt support services

### Business Failure
If your sole trader business has failed, you still owe the tax. HMRC doesn't write off Self-Assessment debt unless you're bankrupt.

**Options:**
- Set up a long-term payment plan (18-24 months in extreme cases)
- Consider bankruptcy or Individual Voluntary Arrangement (IVA) if debts are over £10,000 and you have no assets

### Disputed Tax Bill
If you think HMRC's calculation is wrong:
1. Pay what you think you owe immediately (stops interest on that amount)
2. File an appeal for the disputed portion
3. Don't wait for the appeal to resolve — you'll rack up interest if you're wrong
