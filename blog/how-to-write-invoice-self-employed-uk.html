How to Write an Invoice (Self-Employed UK): What to Include + Free Template

Published: 2026-03-09 · For UK freelancers, sole traders, and self-employed

Writing your first invoice can feel awkward — especially if you've never done it before. What do you actually need to include? Is there a legal format? What if you're not VAT registered?

Good news: invoicing is simpler than it looks. This guide covers everything, with a real example and a checklist you can follow every time.

What must be on your invoice

✅ Every UK invoice should include:
  • Your name (or business name)
  • Your contact details (address, email, phone)
  • Client's name and address
  • A unique invoice number
  • The invoice date
  • A clear description of what you provided
  • The amount due (with currency)
  • Payment terms (when it's due)
  • Your payment details (bank account, sort code, or PayPal)

If you're VAT registered, you must also include:

  • Your VAT registration number
  • The VAT rate applied
  • The VAT amount
  • The total including VAT

Step-by-step: writing your invoice

1. Choose an invoice number system

Your invoice numbers must be unique and sequential. Common formats:

  • Simple sequential: 001, 002, 003...
  • Year-based: 2026-001, 2026-002...
  • Initials + year: JS-2026-001
  • Client-based: TECH-001, TECH-002...
💡 Tip: Don't start at 001 if you'd rather not advertise that you're brand new. Starting at 100 or 1001 is perfectly fine and common practice.

2. Add your details

Include your full name (or registered business name), address, email, and phone number. This tells the client exactly who they're paying.

If you work from home and don't want to share your home address, consider a PO Box or virtual office address.

3. Add the client's details

Their company name, address, and a contact email. If they have a specific "accounts" or "finance" email, use that — it speeds up payment processing.

4. Describe what you did

Be specific. "Copywriting services" is vague. "Website copy — homepage, about page, services page (3 pages × £250)" is clear and hard to dispute.

Good descriptions include:

  • What you delivered
  • Quantity (hours, pages, items, days)
  • The rate per unit
  • The line total

5. Set clear payment terms

State when the invoice is due. Common terms:

  • Due on receipt — payment expected immediately
  • Net 14 — due within 14 days
  • Net 30 — due within 30 days (most common)
  • Net 60 — due within 60 days (avoid if you can)

Include the actual due date, not just "Net 30." Writing "Due: 8 April 2026" removes any ambiguity.

6. Add your payment details

Make it as easy as possible for them to pay you:

  • Bank transfer: sort code, account number, account name, payment reference
  • PayPal: your PayPal email
  • Stripe/card link: include a payment URL if you have one
💡 Pro tip: Always include a payment reference (usually the invoice number). This makes reconciliation much easier when you're doing your self-assessment.

7. Add a late payment notice (optional but recommended)

A simple line at the bottom referencing the Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998 reminds clients that late payment has consequences. You don't need to threaten — just state the fact.

Example: "Late payments may incur statutory interest at 8% + Bank of England base rate, plus fixed compensation of £40-£100 under the Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998."

Common invoicing mistakes

  • No invoice number — makes it impossible to reference later and fails HMRC record-keeping requirements
  • Vague descriptions — "services rendered" invites disputes. Be specific.
  • No due date — if you don't say when it's due, the client decides
  • Missing payment details — every extra step for the client delays payment
  • Adding VAT when not registered — you cannot charge VAT unless you have a VAT registration number
  • Inconsistent formatting — professional, consistent invoices get paid faster
  • Sending to the wrong person — always confirm who handles payments

Do you need invoicing software?

Not necessarily. You can create invoices using:

  • Word/Google Docs — fine for low volume, but manual
  • Excel/Google Sheets — good for tracking, but formatting is fiddly
  • Free tools — our free invoice generator creates professional invoices instantly
  • Accounting software — FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks (from ~£12/month) — best if you send 10+ invoices per month

Start simple. Don't pay for software until your invoicing volume justifies it.

What about recurring invoices?

If you invoice the same client monthly for the same amount (retainer, subscription, ongoing work), set up a template and change only the date and invoice number each month.

Better yet, use a tool that generates recurring invoices automatically. Most accounting software can do this.

When to send your invoice

The sooner you invoice, the sooner you get paid. Best practices:

  • On completion — send the invoice the same day you deliver the work
  • At milestones — for larger projects, invoice at each agreed stage
  • Monthly — for ongoing work, invoice at the start of each month for the previous month's work
  • Upfront — for new clients or high-value projects, ask for a deposit invoice before starting
⚠️ Don't wait. Every week you delay sending an invoice adds a week to your payment timeline. Freelancers who invoice on the same day they deliver get paid an average of 2 weeks faster.

Keeping records

HMRC requires you to keep copies of all invoices for at least 5 years after the relevant tax year. Keep:

  • A copy of every invoice (PDF or digital)
  • A log of invoice numbers, amounts, dates, and payment status
  • Records of any credit notes or adjustments
