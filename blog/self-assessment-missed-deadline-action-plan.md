# Missed Your Self-Assessment Deadline? Your 7-Day Action Plan

*Last updated: 24 February 2026*

If you've missed the 31st January Self-Assessment deadline, you're not alone. Over 700,000 people miss it every year. The key now isn't panic—it's action.

**The faster you act, the less you'll pay in penalties.**

This guide walks you through exactly what to do, day by day, to minimise damage and get compliant.

---

## What You're Facing (Reality Check)

Missing the Self-Assessment deadline triggers an automatic penalty cascade:

- **£100 penalty** – arrives immediately (even if you owe £0 tax)
- **Daily penalties** – £10/day from 1st May (if still not filed after 3 months)
- **6-month penalty** – Greater of £300 or 5% of tax owed
- **12-month penalty** – Another £300 or 5% of tax owed
- **Interest** – 7.75% APR on unpaid tax from 1st February

**Example:** If you owe £3,000 in tax and file in June:
- £100 immediate penalty
- £310 daily penalties (31 days × £10)
- £232.50 interest (3,000 × 7.75% × 4/12)
- **Total cost: £642.50** on top of your £3,000 tax bill

The good news? Most of this is avoidable if you act now.

## Your 7-Day Action Plan

### Day 1: Register & Gather Documents

**Morning (2 hours):**

1. **If not registered:** [Register for Self-Assessment](https://www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment) immediately
   - HMRC sends your UTR (Unique Taxpayer Reference) within 10 days by post
   - You need this to file, so don't delay

2. **If already registered:** Locate your UTR and Government Gateway login
   - Lost your UTR? [Request it online](https://www.gov.uk/find-lost-utr-number)
   - Forgotten Gateway password? Reset it now

3. **Gather your income documents:**
   - Bank statements (all accounts used for business)
   - Sales invoices or payment records
   - Purchase receipts (expenses you can claim)
   - P60/P45 if you have employed income
   - Dividend vouchers or investment statements

**Afternoon (1 hour):**

Create a simple spreadsheet with three columns:
- Income sources (client payments, sales, interest, etc.)
- Business expenses (software, equipment, travel, etc.)
- Personal tax payments already made (PAYE, payments on account)

Don't worry about perfection—get the numbers roughly right. You can amend the return later if needed.

**Result:** By end of Day 1, you should have all paperwork in one place and know roughly what you earned/spent.

---

### Day 2: Calculate Your Income & Expenses

**Task:** Turn your documents into actual figures.

**For freelancers/sole traders:**

1. **Total your income:**
   - Add up all client payments received between 6 April 2024 – 5 April 2025
   - Include cash payments, bank transfers, PayPal, Stripe
   - Don't forget one-off projects or side gigs

2. **Total your allowable expenses:**
   - Office costs (software, equipment, stationery)
   - Travel (business mileage at 45p/mile for first 10k miles)
   - Professional fees (accountant, subscriptions)
   - Marketing (website, ads, business cards)
   - Training (courses, books related to your work)

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- ❌ Claiming personal expenses (your gym membership isn't allowable)
- ❌ Forgetting cash payments you received
- ❌ Mixing business and personal spending (HMRC hates this)

**For multiple income streams:**

Calculate each separately:
- Self-employment income
- Rental income (property)
- Investment income (dividends, interest)
- Foreign income

**Result:** By end of Day 2, you should have clear figures: Total Income and Total Allowable Expenses.

### Day 3: Use HMRC's Online Service or Software

**Option 1: HMRC's Free Online Service**

Best for simple returns (just self-employment income, no complexity).

1. Log into your [Government Gateway account](https://www.gov.uk/log-in-file-self-assessment-tax-return)
2. Click "File your Self-Assessment tax return"
3. Follow the step-by-step questions
4. Enter your income and expenses from Day 2
5. HMRC calculates your tax automatically

**Option 2: Commercial Software**

Required for complex returns (rental income, multiple businesses, partnerships).

Recommended tools:
- **TaxCalc** – £75/year (good for sole traders)
- **QuickBooks Self-Employed** – £10/month (tracks expenses year-round)
- **FreeAgent** – £19/month (freelancer-focused)

All integrate with HMRC and submit your return electronically.

**If you're overwhelmed:**

Consider hiring an accountant for this year (£150-400). They can:
- File your return correctly
- Maximise your allowable expenses
- Advise on avoiding penalties next year

**Result:** By end of Day 3, you should be logged into HMRC or have software set up.

---

### Day 4: Complete Your Tax Return

**Block out 3-4 hours.** Don't rush this.

**Section-by-section walkthrough:**

**1. Personal details**
- Name, address, UTR, National Insurance number
- These should pre-fill if you've filed before

**2. Self-employment (SA103 form)**
- Business description (e.g., "Freelance graphic designer")
- Start date (when you began trading)
- Turnover (total income before expenses)
- Allowable expenses (from your Day 2 spreadsheet)
- Net profit (turnover minus expenses)

**3. Other income**
- Employment (if you also have PAYE work)
- Savings interest
- Dividends
- Rental income
- State benefits

**4. Tax reliefs**
- Pension contributions (deduct these—they reduce your tax)
- Gift Aid donations
- Marriage Allowance (if applicable)

**5. Student loan**
- Select your plan type (Plan 1/2/4/Postgraduate)
- HMRC will calculate repayments automatically

**Common mistakes:**
- Forgetting to claim capital allowances (equipment over £1,000)
- Not declaring cash income (HMRC *will* find out)
- Mixing tax years (this is 2024/25, not last year)

**Result:** By end of Day 4, your return should be 90% complete. Sleep on it before submitting.

---

### Day 5: Review & Submit

**Morning: Final checks**

1. Review every figure
   - Does your income match your bank statements?
   - Did you claim all allowable expenses?
   - Are National Insurance and student loan sections correct?

2. Check the tax calculation
   - Does it look reasonable?
   - If your bill seems way too high/low, double-check your figures

3. Look for red flags HMRC hates:
   - Round numbers (£10,000 expenses looks suspicious)
   - Expenses higher than income
   - Missing income sources

**Afternoon: Submit**

1. Click "Submit your return"
2. HMRC will show you a summary—read it carefully
3. Confirm and submit
4. **Save your submission receipt**—you'll need this if HMRC queries anything

**Immediate result:**
- Your return is filed ✅
- You'll receive a confirmation email
- Your tax bill is now official

**Next:** HMRC will send a formal calculation (SA302) within 72 hours.

### Day 6: Address the £100 Penalty

**The automatic penalty**

You'll receive a penalty notice for £100 within 4-6 weeks. This is automatic—everyone who files late gets it, even if you owe £0 tax.

**Can you appeal it?**

Yes, but only if you have a "reasonable excuse":
- ✅ Serious illness (hospital admission, not just a cold)
- ✅ Bereavement (close family member)
- ✅ Fire/flood/theft affecting your records
- ✅ HMRC system outage on deadline day
- ✅ Unexpected postal delays (if you filed by post)
- ❌ Busy at work
- ❌ Forgot the deadline
- ❌ Didn't know you needed to file

**If you have a reasonable excuse:**

1. Write to HMRC within 30 days of the penalty notice
2. Explain your circumstances clearly
3. Attach evidence (doctor's note, death certificate, etc.)
4. Request penalty cancellation

**Template appeal letter:**

> HM Revenue and Customs  
> Self-Assessment  
> BX9 1AS
> 
> Re: Appeal against £100 late-filing penalty  
> UTR: [your 10-digit number]  
> Tax year: 2024/25
> 
> Dear Sir/Madam,
> 
> I am writing to appeal the £100 late-filing penalty for the above tax year.
> 
> I have a reasonable excuse for filing late: [explain your circumstances in 2-3 sentences].
> 
> I have now filed my return (submitted on [date]) and paid the tax owed in full.
> 
> I attach [evidence] in support of this appeal.
> 
> I request that you cancel the penalty on the grounds of reasonable excuse.
> 
> Yours faithfully,  
> [Your name]

**If you don't have a reasonable excuse:**

Pay the £100 when it arrives. Don't ignore it—additional penalties apply if you don't pay within 30 days.

### Day 7: Set Up Payment & Avoid Future Penalties

**Pay your tax bill**

You have until **31st July 2026** to pay your 2024/25 tax bill.

**Payment options:**

1. **Bank transfer** (fastest)
   - HMRC bank details are in your tax calculation
   - Use your UTR as the reference
   - Takes 3 working days to clear

2. **Direct Debit**
   - Set up via your Government Gateway
   - Can spread payment over 12 months (no interest!)
   - Must set up by 31st May 2026

3. **Debit/credit card**
   - Pay online via HMRC website
   - Instant confirmation

**If you can't afford to pay:**

Don't ignore it. Contact HMRC immediately:
- Call the Payment Support Service: 0300 200 3822
- Explain your situation
- Request a Time to Pay arrangement (monthly instalments)

HMRC will agree to instalments if:
- You've filed your return
- You're honest about your finances
- You stick to the agreed payment plan

**Avoid next year's penalties:**

1. **Set up quarterly reminders** (7 July, 7 October, 7 January, 7 April)
   - These are Self-Assessment key dates

2. **Track income/expenses monthly** (not once a year)
   - Use QuickBooks, FreeAgent, or a simple spreadsheet

3. **Register for Making Tax Digital** (if turnover >£50k)
   - Mandatory from April 2026 for some traders
   - Check if it applies to you: [MTD Readiness Checker](/tools/mtd-readiness-checker)

4. **Consider paying on account**
   - HMRC may ask you to pay tax in advance (two instalments: 31 Jan & 31 July)
   - This spreads the cost but can be confusing
   - Budget for it now

**Result:** By end of Day 7, you're compliant, you've addressed the penalty, and you have a payment plan.

## What Happens Next?

**Short term (next 4 weeks):**
- HMRC sends formal SA302 tax calculation (keep this—you'll need it for mortgage applications)
- Penalty notice arrives (£100)
- If you appealed, HMRC responds within 45 days

**Medium term (next 3 months):**
- If you haven't paid your tax by 31st July, interest continues to accrue
- If you haven't filed by 1st May, daily penalties start (£10/day)

**Long term (next 12 months):**
- HMRC may investigate if they spot errors in your return
- You may receive a "nudge letter" asking you to check something
- Payments on account may be due (31 Jan & 31 July 2027)

**Best case scenario:**
- You file in the next few days → pay £100 penalty → pay tax by 31 July → done
- Total penalty: £100 (vs. £700+ if you'd delayed until summer)
